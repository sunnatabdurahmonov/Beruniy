Central Asian scientists such as Khorezmi, Farghani, Habash Khasib, Marvaridi worked in various fields in the Academy of Sciences of that time named "Donishmandlik Uyi" established in Baghdad. Beruni was also a member of this "House of Wisdom" and worked there for 7 years. According to him, according to the order of Caliph Ma'mun, scientists of the "House of Wisdom" were divided into two groups and began to determine the size of the Earth based on the degree measurement method in the Sanjar desert west of Mosul, Iraq. Each group calculated the length of one degree meridian arc based on the results they measured. According to the calculations of the first group, the length of one degree meridian arc is equal to 111,815 kilometers, the value found by the second group was 1315 meters less. Beruni says that the reason for this difference between the results is two different measurements, and he is interested in checking these results and wants to carry out degree measurement work.

For this, he chose the steppe of Dehistan (the southeastern part of the Caspian Sea), but he could not do it because he did not have an assistant and sufficient funds. To determine the size of the earth by the degree measurement method, a flat area of ​​a certain latitude is needed, and several tens of kilometers must be accurately measured here. This work required a lot of time, a lot of money and careful preparation. About this, Beruni said: "The length of the Earth's circumference can be determined as follows without traveling through the desert. For this purpose, by climbing a high mountain on the seashore or in a flat area, before sunrise or sunset, when half of the sun's halo is between the horizon, its angle of descent is measured with a circular armillary instrument. Explains the method of measuring horizon dip using graphs and formulas. In this case, the angle formed by the line of sight with the horizontal line passing through the top of the mountain, depending on the sunrise or sunset, is measured.

Abu Rayhan Beruni used a new method to measure the size of the Earth and calculated that the length of one degree meridian arc is 111.16 kilometers. Beruni accurately measured the radius of the Earth for that period. He calculated the length of the Earth's meridian circle to be 40,183 kilometers. During one of his trips to India, Sultan Mahmud Ghaznavi took Beruni with him, and on the way he had to stay temporarily in the city of Molton in eastern Pakistan. Despite being in a foreign country, he took the opportunity to measure the Earth's surface at Nandna Fort, 400 kilometers west of Molton, first measuring the geographical latitude of the place and finding it to be 320001. It then measures the angle of dip of the horizon. The scientist himself writes about it as follows: "I had to live in the Nandna fortress in India. I saw a high mountain on the west side of the fortress, and a wide desert on the south side, and at that moment I began to try (the method of measuring the dip of the horizon). From the top of the mountain, I clearly saw that the earth was connected with the blue sky. The line of sight descends 00 341 from a line perpendicular to the vertical. I measured the height of the mountain perpendicularly, and it was equal to 652,055 longi (gaz) in the measure used here.'

By his own method, Beruni calculated that the length of the radius of the Earth's sphere is equal to 12803337.036 gas. If we consider that one gas is 0.4933 meters, the length of the radius of the Earth's sphere, according to Beruni, is 6315.886 kilometers. This number is very little different from the value obtained at the present time, that is, the error is 0.9% of the length of the radius. Then Beruni calculates that the length of one degree meridian arc of this 320,001 latitude is 223,550.329 gas or 110,277 kilometers. If we compare this number with the current value of one degree of arc of 110,885 kilometers, it will be known that Beruni made a mistake of only 618 meters in calculating the length of one degree of arc a thousand years ago. Determining the length of the Earth's circumference, as Beruni said, is more accurate and convenient than determining the length of 10 arcs by wandering through the deserts. Therefore, using trigonometric methods, he achieved a more accurate result than previous astronomers in measuring the height of the mountain. Beruni says the following about finding the length of the Earth's circumference: "In order to find the length of the Earth's circumference, in all cases, double the radius, multiply by 22, and divide the resulting product by 7, and the length of the Earth's circumference will be obtained in the units you measured."
